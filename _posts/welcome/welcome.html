What I read in 2020: My ratings versus Goodreads

How well do my book ratings compare to the Goodreads community ratings?

Gary Lynch https://olytico.com/ (Olytico)https://olytico.com/
01-01-2021

Introduction

2020 ended up being a uniquely good year for reading (at least for me). A series of lockdown months helped to reduce the number of distractions I was exposed to. There wasn’t much rugby going on, cinemas were closed and pubs rapidly became a distant memory.

As I took a look back at what I read in 2020, it struck me that it might be interesting to see whether the wider reading public had the similar views on what I’ve read. The best proxy I could find for the wider reader consensus on a book was the average review scores from Goodreads.com. I plotted my personal ratings against the

Each point on the graph represents a book, with the y-axis showing my rating and the x-axis gives the Goodreads rating. Any point that sits on the line represents a book where my rating and the Goodreads rating matched. You can hover over any point to see it’s title and author.

As you can see, only 2 of 40 books had a matched rating. That said, most of the ratings were fairly close, save for a few notable exceptions. Those were the books I was most interested in - where I had a significantly different opinion from the group consensus. Below I’ve pulled out the books with the greatest difference from the Goodreads rating. They’re split into books I significantly preferred and books that I was significantly less keen on.

The Memory Police: This was hands down the best book I read in 2020. Definitely not for everyone, which I think explains the big difference in ratings. If you enjoy allegorical fiction or existential storytelling, I can recommend.

If Cats Disappeared from the World: This is one of those books that sneaks up on you. It begins in a lighthearted manner and slowly reveals its emotional core as you go. I was genuinely surprised its rating wasn’t higher.

Normal People: So good that its now a major television series. The book hit very close to home for me, not least because its set in the university I attended. Worth reading for the culture impact alone - or you could just watch the Tv show.

The Color Purple: A classic for a reason. This moved me more than anything else this year and feels very relevant to current day issues for a novel from the 1980’s.

Frankenstein: A classic with a much lower Goodreads rating than I expected. A landmark novel that is surpisingly readable for something written over 200 years ago.
